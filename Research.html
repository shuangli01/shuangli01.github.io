Research Statement

My long-term research goal is to develop human-centric AI that continuously understands an evolving world and supports human decision-making with timely, interpretable, and actionable intelligence.

My earlier Ph.D. and postdoctoral research established the methodological foundation in continuous-time sequential modeling for irregular event streams, spanning temporal point processes, latent-state models, and sequential decision methods.

My recent work at CUHK-Shenzhen builds on this foundation, moving from modeling when and what events occur to understanding what event sequences reveal about human behavior. I view sequences as behavioral traces of actions, preferences, decisions, and interactions, and develop interpretable models that predict behavior, uncover mechanisms, and support decisions aligned with human needs. This agenda advances along three connected directions.

1.Turn behavioral traces into interpretable temporal mechanisms

Most sequence models predict the next event without explaining why it occurs or how it relates to an underlying behavioral process. Building on temporal point processes, I develop models that combine statistical learning with symbolic rules, structured domain knowledge, and forward-chaining inference. These models capture irregular event dynamics, infer unobserved states, and recover temporal logic that can be inspected, tested, and revised. I further extend this work toward cognitively inspired models of behavior, studying how people form internal world models, reuse learned habits, and switch between fast intuitive responses and slower deliberative reasoning. This direction shifts the objective from next-event prediction toward mechanism discovery, providing a foundation for understanding evolving systems such as clinical trajectories, treatment policies, and human action patterns.

2.Uncover the preference mechanisms behind human decisions

Whereas the first direction explains how behavior evolves over time, this direction studies how context shapes what people choose and value. Choices are rarely made in isolation: the attractiveness of an option can depend on the available alternatives, prior interactions, framing, and changing individual preferences. I develop interpretable neural and symbolic choice models that capture these context effects, interactions among options, behavioral heterogeneity, and preference evolution. I also study the statistical and optimization questions that arise when such models are used to design assortments, recommendations, and other decision-support interventions. This direction connects sequential behavior modeling with choice and preference learning, enabling AI systems to anticipate not only what people will do, but also what outcomes they value.

3.Design collaborative AI that aligns with human goals while recognizing human blind spots

Rather than viewing AI as an autonomous replacement for human expertise, I study how it can serve as a complementary decision partner while recognizing human blind spots. Building on temporal mechanisms and preference models, this line investigates how AI should recommend, question, and coordinate with humans when decisions unfold over time. I develop algorithms that align predictions, explanations, and interventions with human intentions, preferences, and reasoning while accounting for uncertainty, bias, and cognitive limitations. In clinical decision support and other high-stakes settings, this work turns interpretable models into collaborative partners that know when to recommend, when to deliberate, and whom to consult.

Together, these directions form a coherent research agenda that moves from event prediction to human understanding and, ultimately, to human-aligned AI support that is interpretable, adaptive, and useful for decisions that matter.

Research arc Event prediction→ Temporal mechanisms→ Preference mechanisms→ Human-aligned AI support
